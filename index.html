The Treaty of Waitangi, signed on February 6, 1840, is a foundational document in New Zealand's history, marking the beginning of British colonization and the establishment of a partnership between the indigenous Māori people and the British Crown. The treaty was signed between representatives of the British Crown, including William Hobson, the Lieutenant Governor of New Zealand, and various Māori chiefs from different tribes.

The treaty consists of three main articles, with the English and Māori versions having some differences in interpretation. The first article, often referred to as the "principles of partnership," guarantees Māori sovereignty and the right to govern their own affairs, while also ceding governance to the British Crown. The second article grants Māori the same rights and privileges as British subjects, while the third article establishes British protection over Māori lands, ensuring that they could only be sold to the Crown.

However, interpretations and understandings of the treaty have been the subject of debate and contention over the years. Many Māori argue that the treaty was misrepresented and that the Māori version differs significantly from the English one, leading to misunderstandings and breaches of trust. Issues such as land confiscation, cultural assimilation, and loss of autonomy have been ongoing points of tension between Māori and the New Zealand government.

In recent decades, there has been a concerted effort to address historical grievances and honor the treaty's principles of partnership and protection. The Waitangi Tribunal, established in 1975, has been instrumental in investigating breaches of the treaty and recommending settlements to address injustices suffered by Māori. The treaty continues to play a central role in New Zealand's legal and political landscape, serving as a framework for reconciliation and the recognition of Māori rights and aspirations.
